3D printed Warhammer models have a secret advantage over injection-moulded plastic: the surface can be *smoother* and more detailed than GW's own kits. But that smooth surface needs proper preparation or paint will slide right off. This guide covers everything from raw print to competition-level paintwork.

## Why Resin Behaves Differently

Uncured resin residue left on models repels acrylic paint. Even after washing, a light film can remain invisible to the eye but deadly to paint adhesion. This is why so many hobbyists report paint "chipping off within days" on their first resin prints.

The fix is simple: a proper cure-and-prime workflow.

## Step 1: Thorough Washing

Never skip this step. Remove your print from the build plate with gloves on (uncured resin is a skin irritant).

**Two-bucket method:**
- Bucket 1: 90%+ IPA, 3 minutes with gentle agitation
- Bucket 2: Fresh IPA, 1–2 minutes rinse

Alternatively, a **sonic cleaner** with IPA produces the cleanest results. For large Warhammer models (vehicles, Daemon Primarchs), 5 minutes in each bucket.

## Step 2: Full UV Cure

Cure for **6–8 minutes per side** under UV light. For complex models like Space Marine Dreadnoughts, rotate and cure 4 times. An under-cured model will feel slightly tacky and will never take paint well.

Our professional curing station at Arcane Flame uses industrial UV boxes at 405nm — the same wavelength used by our printers. All our shipped models are fully cured to this standard.

## Step 3: Sanding (Optional but Recommended)

If your model was printed at 0.05mm+ layer height, visible layer lines on flat surfaces (vehicle panels, flat armour) should be sanded:

- **320 grit**: Remove obvious lines (wet sand with water to reduce dust)
- **600 grit**: Smooth the surface
- **800 grit**: Final pass before priming

For organic shapes like Space Marine armour curves, layer lines are barely visible and may not need sanding at all.

## Step 4: Priming

This is the most important step. Use a **spray primer designed for plastic models**:

- **Vallejo Surface Primer** (our top pick) — excellent adhesion, no obscuring detail
- **Citadel Chaos Black** — reliable but can be thick in warm weather
- **Tamiya Fine Surface Primer** — great for smooth vehicles

Apply in **thin coats** from 25–30cm distance. Avoid thick gloopy coats that fill detail.

**Zenithal priming technique** (used by 'Eavy Metal painters and pro painters worldwide):

1. Black primer all over — creates deep shadows
2. Dark grey from a 45° angle
3. White/light grey from directly above — creates instant shading

This technique makes the next stages dramatically easier.

## Step 5: Base Coating

For Warhammer Space Marines, the classic Ultramarines scheme:

1. **Base**: Macragge Blue (brush or airbrush)
2. **Gold trim**: Retributor Armour
3. **Weapons**: Abaddon Black
4. **Eyes**: Mephiston Red

Thinning paints with 2–3 drops of water per drop of paint is non-negotiable. Thick paint is the enemy of detail.

## Step 6: Shading (The Magic Step)

A good shade wash transforms a flat base coat into a three-dimensional model instantly.

- **Nuln Oil** for dark armour, metals, and recesses
- **Agrax Earthshade** for browns, bone, leather, and a warm tone overall
- **Reikland Fleshshade** for skin tones

Apply with a large brush directly into recesses. The wash flows into cracks and deepens shadow. Let dry fully (30–40 minutes).

## Step 7: Layering and Highlights

Edge highlighting is what separates "tabletop quality" from "display quality":

1. **First highlight**: Mix base colour 50/50 with a lighter tone, apply to raised edges
2. **Second highlight**: Pure lighter colour on uppermost edges only
3. **Extreme highlight**: Near-white on sharpest edges for dramatic effect

For a Space Marine in Macragge Blue:
- Caledor Sky on upper armour plates
- Fenrisian Grey on the extreme edges
- White on the very tips

## Results

A properly prepared and painted 3D printed Warhammer model is **indistinguishable** from an injection-moulded kit once paint is applied. In many cases, the sharper detail from ultra-detail resin printing makes models look *better*.
